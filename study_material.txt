Photosynthesis is the biological process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria convert sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide into oxygen and energy-rich sugars. 
It serves as the primary energy foundation for nearly all life on Earth and is responsible for producing the oxygen we breathe.
The Chemical ProcessAt its core, photosynthesis transforms light energy into chemical energy. 
The overall balanced chemical equation is:
    6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Light Energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂Reactants: 
    Six carbon dioxide molecules (CO₂) from the air and six water molecules (H₂O) absorbed by the roots.
    Products: One molecule of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), which the organism uses for food and growth, and six molecules of oxygen (O₂), which are released into the atmosphere as a byproduct.
The Two Main StagesIn plants, this process occurs in the chloroplasts using a green pigment called chlorophyll, which captures the sunlight. 
It is divided into two distinct phases:Light-Dependent Reactions: 
    These take place in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplast. 
    Sunlight splits water molecules into oxygen, protons, and electrons. 
    This captures the light energy and converts it into usable cellular energy (ATP and NADPH).
    Light-Independent Reactions (The Calvin Cycle): 
        These occur in the stroma (fluid) of the chloroplast and do not directly require light. 
        They use the ATP and NADPH generated in the first stage to convert carbon dioxide into sugars.
